ABSTRACT 

Continual post-training is becoming a central paradigm for adapting vision-language models to evolving tasks. Recent work has increasingly favored rein-forcement learning over supervised fine-tuning, driven by the belief that reinforce-ment learning is inherently less prone to forgetting. However, the belief remains insufficiently validated, as existing evidence is largely drawn from outdated or ho-mogeneous benchmarks. To revisit this assumption, we introduce MRCL, a Mul-timodal Reasoning Continual Learning benchmark built from diverse and recently released multimodal datasets. Experiments on MRCL show that reinforcement learning can still suffer from severe catastrophic forgetting during continual post-training. To address this challenge, we propose Continual Policy Optimization (CPO), a replay-free framework grounded in the prior-task behavioral KL ob-jective. CPO uses a theoretically justified parameter-movement regularization to limit policy drift on previous tasks. Extensive experiments across multiple model scales demonstrate that CPO consistently reduces forgetting while preserving, and in some cases improving, pretrained model capabilities. On Qwen3-VL-8B, CPO reduces forgetting by 13.7% and improves pretrained capability by 7.0%. The implementation code is available at https://github.com/MaolinLuo/CPO. 

1 INTRODUCTION 

Vision-language models (VLMs) have demonstrated strong performance across a wide range of real-world tasks (Comanici et al., 2025; Achiam et al., 2023; Radford et al., 2021). As VLMs are increasingly deployed in dynamic environments, they require continual adaptation to evolving data and tasks. However, such a process is hindered by the fundamental challenge of catastrophic forget-ting (French, 1999), where learning new tasks degrades previously acquired capabilities. Previous studies mainly address the problem under supervised fine-tuning (SFT) (Gu et al., 2026a; Chen et al., 2025b; Kurniawan et al., 2024). Recently, reinforcement learning (RL) has emerged as a promising alternative for continual post-training (Lai et al., 2025). Several studies (Shenfeld et al., 2026; Zhang et al., 2026; Jin et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025a) report that RL induces substantially less forgetting than SFT. These findings raise an important question: is RL intrinsically resistant to forgetting in continual VLM post-training? 

We show that the current evidence is incomplete. Existing evaluations often rely on outdated or nar-row benchmarks, which may underestimate the severity of forgetting. For example, Lai et al. (2025) train on datasets released between 2018 and 2023, while using Qwen2.5-VL (Bai et al., 2025b), a model released in 2025. Such datasets may overlap with modern pretraining corpora, making the corresponding capabilities easier to retain. Other studies evaluate RL on limited task distributions; for instance, Zhang et al. (2026) draw conclusions from a single jigsaw task. To provide a more reliable evaluation, we introduce MRCL, a Multimodal Reasoning Continual Learning benchmark

Experiments on MRCL reveal that continual RL still suffers from severe catastrophic forgetting as shown in Fig. 1, challenging the view that RL naturally solves continual learning. We examine why existing RL objectives fail to prevent forgetting. The key issue is an objective mismatch. Shenfeld et al. (2026) argue that the KL regularization term in GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) can directly reflect forgetting, whereas Lai et al. (2025) and Chen et al. (2025a) suggest that KL regularization is not the main factor behind reduced forgetting. Our experiments further show that the GRPO KL constraint does not effectively mitigate forgetting; instead, it can reduce model plasticity when learning new target tasks. As GRPO computes the KL term on current-task data, 

Ex‚àºT new [KL ( œÄŒ∏ ‚à•œÄold )] , it is mainly designed to prevent over-optimization (Shao et al., 2024). Recent methods such as DAPO (Yu et al., 2025) and GSPO (Zheng et al., 2025) remove the KL term to improve learning capacity. However, catastrophic forgetting is caused by behavioral drift on previously learned tasks. Our theoretical analysis demonstrates that the constraint on current policy should instead be evaluated on data from prior task Ex‚àºT old [KL ( œÄŒ∏ ‚à•œÄold )] . The current-task KL in GRPO can serve as a proxy to prevent forgetting only when the distribution is closed to the prior-task distribution. In continual learning, however, prior-task data are often unavailable due to efficiency constraints and data privacy concerns (Wang et al., 2024b; Zhou et al., 2024; Rolnick et al., 2019), which motivates a replay-free mechanism for constraining prior-task behavioral drift. To constrain the prior-task KL without accessing Told , a classical approach is to approximate it in parameter space. EWC (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017) follows the idea by using a second-order approx-imation with the empirical Fisher to identify and regularize important parameters. However, esti-mating the Fisher requires additional gradient computation over replayed data and incurs substantial memory overhead, making it impractical for large-scale post-training. Theoretically, we show that the oracle prior-task KL can instead be locally relaxed to an empirical parameter movement measure accumulated during prior training. Based on the insight, we propose Continual Policy Optimization (CPO), which maintains a cumulative set of high-movement parameters and applies masked sparse movement regularization to the protected subset during sequential policy optimization. CPO theoretically relaxes the intractable historical KL constraint into a parameter-movement surrogate to preserve prior-task behavior without storing old data. ‚Ä¢ Extensive experiments across multiple VLM scales demonstrate that CPO substantially reduces forgetting. For MedBookVQA and Puzzle , a prediction receives a score of 1 for an exact match and 0 otherwise. We conduct the experiments over a 5-stage continual learning process shown in Tab. 2, 3 and 4, treating each dataset as an individual task. The results demonstrate that our proposed method yields increasingly significant improvements as the model scale grows, indicating a strong scaling capabil-ity. Specifically, on Qwen3-VL-8B, our proposed CPO achieves a mean final accuracy of 75.46%, which represents a 13.67% improvement over GSPO while maintaining a high mean finetune accu-racy of 74.80%. This performance trend confirms that our parameter-movement constraint scales exceptionally well to larger models. In comparison, SFT baselines suffer from catastrophic forget-ting across all model sizes, as seen in the drop in mean final accuracy to 43.99% for full SFT on the Qwen3-VL-8B model. Although standard RL methods generally preserve more prior knowledge than SFT, they still undergo significant performance decay over extended task sequences. Fur-thermore, adding standard KL penalties on current-task data, as evaluated in GRPO*, restricts the adaptation ability on new tasks without effectively preventing the forgetting of older capabilities. By contrast, CPO resolves this trade-off by successfully mitigating policy drift on prior tasks without compromising learning efficiency. Reinforcement learning aims to optimize models based on reward signals, which has proven partic-ularly beneficial for eliciting deep cognitive reasoning capabilities, commonly referred to as System 2 thinking. In the context of large models, post-training RL has witnessed rapid evolution from Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017) to Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) (Shao et al., 2024), followed by further algorithmic improvements such as DAPO (Yu et al., 2025) and GSPO (Zheng et al., 2025). Consequently, there is growing interest in understanding the capability of RL to support continual learning (Shenfeld et al., 2026; Abel et al., 2023). A series of recent studies (Shenfeld et al., 2026; Zhang et al., 2026; Chen et al., 2025a; Lai et al., 2025) show that RL naturally exhibits stronger resistance to catastrophic forgetting compared to SFT. Furthermore, Jin et al. (2025) found that RL fine-tuning can heal out-of-distribution forgetting previously induced by SFT. However, the underlying mechanisms behind the robustness remain highly debated. Shenfeld et al. (2026) empirically attributes the resistance to forgetting to the KL divergence penalty inherent in standard RL algorithms such as GRPO (Shao et al., 2024), while empirical evaluations by Chen et al. (2025a) and Lai et al. (2025) demonstrate that KL regularization plays a negligible role in preventing forgetting. Instead, Zhang et al. (2026) and Chen et al. (2025a) argue that the retention of prior knowledge stems from the on-policy data generation process during RL, rather than intrinsic algorithmic differences. A recent study (Lou et al., 2026) highlights that RL still suffers from non-negligible forgetting on classification tasks. Despite these observations, how catastrophic forgetting occurs in complex System 2 reasoning tasks where modern RL is most typically applied. And the fundamental essence of the forgetting phe-nomenon in continual RL remains largely unexplored. 

6 CONCLUSION 

In this work, we revisit the assumption that reinforcement learning is inherently robust to forget-ting in continual VLM post-training. We introduce MRCL, a Multimodal Reasoning Continual Learning benchmark built from recent and diverse multimodal tasks, and show that continual RL still suffers from severe catastrophic forgetting under more realistic evaluation settings. To address this challenge, we propose Continual Policy Optimization, a replay-free framework grounded in the prior-task behavioral KL objective. By relaxing the intractable historical KL constraint into a parameter-movement surrogate, CPO preserves prior-task behavior with negligible additional com-putational and memory overhead, making it scalable to large VLMs. Together, MRCL and CPO provide both a more faithful benchmark and a principled replay-free optimization framework for studying continual post-training in modern vision-language models. 11 Preprint

C ADDITIONAL RESULTS 

Effect of L1 versus L2 movement regularization. We further compare CPO with masked L1

movement regularization against a masked L2 variant on Qwen3-VL-2B. Fig. 4 shows that both reg-ularization terms are active during training, as their losses consistently rise and decay across contin-ual stages. Therefore, the performance gap is not caused by an ineffective L2 regularization signal. Instead, the difference lies in the induced movement pattern. As shown in Fig. 3, masked L2 causes dense movement across many top movement parameters, especially in later stages, whereas masked 

L1 keeps most top movement parameters nearly unchanged and yields a much sparser movement pattern. In terms of continual learning performance, replacing masked L1 with masked L2 decreases MFN and MTA by 23% and 15% , respectively. 20 Preprint

F CASE STUDY 

This section presents a qualitative case study across diverse datasets to illustrate the evolution of model behavior during continual post-training. We compare model responses to identical test sam-ples when evaluated immediately after learning each specific task versus after completing the en-tire sequential training process. The results show that while GSPO successfully acquires correct problem-solving strategies when evaluated immediately after training on a task, it subsequently suf-fers from severe cognitive collapse on those same tasks as the sequence progresses. For example, after finishing the final FinMME stage, the GSPO model fails to maintain mathematical precision, misinterprets grid dimensions in navigation, and generates heavily hallucinated answers on medical 26 Preprint datasets. In contrast, CPO consistently preserves both the step-by-step reasoning structure and the visual comprehension acquired during earlier stages without degrading its logical chain.
